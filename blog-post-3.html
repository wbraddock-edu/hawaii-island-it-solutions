Cybersecurity Basics for Small Businesses

// security · May 10, 2025 · 5 min read

Cybersecurity might sound like a concern for big corporations, but small businesses in Hawaii are actually disproportionately targeted by cybercriminals. Why? Because small businesses often have valuable data (customer information, credit card data, financial records) but lack the dedicated IT security of larger companies. Here's what every Honolulu small business owner needs to know.

Use Strong, Unique Passwords and a Password Manager

The most common entry point for data breaches is still weak or reused passwords. Every account should have a unique, complex password. Using a password manager like 1Password or Bitwarden makes this practical — you only need to remember one strong master password. We set these up for clients as part of our business IT onboarding.

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication Everywhere

Two-factor authentication (2FA) is one of the most effective security measures available. Enable it on every business account: email, banking, QuickBooks, your point-of-sale system. Even if an attacker gets your password, they can't access your account without the second factor.

Keep Software Updated

Software updates contain security patches for known vulnerabilities. Delaying updates is one of the riskiest things a business can do. Configure Windows, macOS, and all business software to update automatically. Don't ignore those "update available" notifications.

Back Up Your Data — The 3-2-1 Rule

Keep three copies of your data: two on different local storage media, one off-site (or in the cloud). This protects against ransomware attacks, hardware failures, and physical disasters (a very real concern in Hawaii given hurricane and flood risks). We set up automated cloud backup solutions for our business clients starting at $15/month.

Train Your Employees

Phishing emails — fraudulent messages designed to steal credentials or install malware — are responsible for the majority of small business breaches. Training your team to recognize suspicious emails, fake login pages, and social engineering attempts is the most cost-effective security investment you can make.
